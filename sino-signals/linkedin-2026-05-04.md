What happens when five Chinese ministries decide AI companions need product-safety rules, not just content rules?

On April 10, the Cyberspace Administration of China, NDRC, MIIT, Ministry of Public Security, and State Administration for Market Regulation jointly issued Order No. 21, the Interim Measures on AI Personified Interactive Services. The rule takes effect July 15, 2026.

Order 21 covers any service that simulates a person's personality, thinking, or communication style for sustained emotional interaction. Providers cannot offer virtual relatives or companions to minors. Users under 14 require parental consent. A pop-up alert must trigger every two hours of continuous use. When a user shows extreme emotional or self-harm signals, providers must intervene and contact guardians or emergency contacts. Services with one million registered users or 100,000 monthly actives must file a safety assessment. Penalties run to 100,000 yuan, 200,000 yuan where life or health is harmed.

China hosts the world's largest companion-AI market. Platforms like Xingye, Zhumeng Dao, and Doubao's character mode aggregate tens of millions of monthly users. Joint sign-off across five ministries pulls enforcement out of pure cyberspace remit and into product safety, public security, and industrial policy in one stroke. Application stores must verify safety assessments before listing.

China is the first major jurisdiction to regulate AI companions as a distinct product category with binding minor-protection, time-use, and emotional-intervention requirements. The EU AI Act treats companion AI under general-purpose risk tiers. The US has no federal equivalent. Western platforms with China exposure face a hard July 15 deadline.
