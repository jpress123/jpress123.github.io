What happens to a creative industry when the cost of making it falls by 95 percent in a single quarter?

China just answered. In Q1 2026, about 128,000 micro dramas were released nationwide, nearly four times the prior-year total, and roughly 122,000 of them, over 95 percent, were AI made. ByteDance's Seedance 2.0 video model, released in February, cut the cost of a 100-minute drama from a 300,000 to 400,000 yuan live-action shoot with a 40-person crew to under 20,000 yuan in three to seven days. After ByteDance's Hongguo platform cut live-action subsidies, nearly 50,000 new AI dramas went online in April alone.

Why it scales in China: the micro-drama market is projected to top 120 billion yuan (16.5 billion US dollars) in 2026, and ByteDance owns the whole chain, from Fanqie Novel scripts to Doubao and Jimeng tools, Volcano Engine cloud, and Hongguo and Douyin distribution reaching over 100 million daily users. One company can flip an industry to AI production at once.

Global impact: this is the world's first mass commercial deployment of AI-generated video. Streaming platform iQiyi has said it wants AI to make most of its new films and TV shows and is targeting a fully AI-generated movie this summer. Western studios and streamers now face a competitor producing watchable serialized content at near computing-power cost.

Monday morning: benchmark your content and marketing production cost base against a sub-20,000-yuan AI pipeline, and decide which formats you can defend on quality, talent, or brand. Have your media teams run a real brief through Chinese video models this quarter so the gap is measured, not assumed.
