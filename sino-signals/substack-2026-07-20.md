# Sino-Signals | Monday, July 20, 2026, Two Rulebooks and One Machine

*China spent the weekend founding a rival AI governance bloc and showing the hardware that makes export controls negotiable.*

The World AI Conference in Shanghai closes today. It opened with two things a Western executive should read together: a new intergovernmental organization with twenty-nine founding members and no Western democracies, and a supercomputer assembled entirely from Chinese parts. One sets the rules. The other supplies the leverage to argue about them.

## China Launches a Rival Global AI Governance Body With 29 Founding Nations

Twenty-nine countries signed the founding agreement for the World Artificial Intelligence Cooperation Organization on July 16, one day before WAIC opened. Signatories include Russia, Indonesia, Brazil, Pakistan, Kazakhstan and Laos. WAICO is permanently headquartered in Shanghai. Xi Jinping delivered the opening keynote on July 17, his first in the conference's nine-year history, and pledged 5,000 AI training places for people from developing countries over the next five years, alongside AI application cooperation centers with ASEAN, the Arab League, the African Union and CELAC. UN Secretary-General Antonio Guterres attended the signing without joining the organization.

**Why China can scale this.** Premier Li Qiang proposed the body at WAIC 2025 and Beijing spent a year converting the proposal into signatures. The membership offer bundles institutional voice with goods most signatories want and lack, namely open-weight models, training programs and affordable compute. Placing the secretariat in Shanghai hands Chinese ministries permanent agenda-setting access to every member.

**Global implications.** There are now two institutional frameworks for AI governance rather than one contested set of norms. Countries aligned to WAICO can draft AI regulation that owes nothing to the EU AI Act, the OECD AI Principles or the G7 Hiroshima Process. Any company selling AI products across both blocs will eventually maintain two compliance tracks written by different authors toward different ends.

**Monday morning.** Ask your general counsel which growth markets signed the charter, and whether your AI compliance program assumes a single global standard.

## Huawei Shows Its 8,192 Chip AI Supercomputer Built Without American Parts

Huawei displayed the physical Atlas 950 SuperPoD for the first time at WAIC. Tang Wenkan, director of the Shanghai Municipal Commission of Economy and Informatization, called it the largest super node in the industry when he previewed the conference lineup on July 7. The system links 8,192 Ascend 950DT processors through Huawei's UnifiedBus 2.0 interconnect, which maps every chip's memory into every other chip's address space so the whole rack behaves as one logical machine. Huawei reports 8 exaflops of FP8 compute and claims 6.7 times the throughput of Nvidia's NVL144. Both figures are self-reported and no third party has benchmarked them at scale.

**Why China can scale this.** MIIT official Gan Xiaobin said AI adoption among China's large-scale industrial enterprises has passed 30 percent, and the country shipped over 100 million AI smart terminals last year. State procurement rules favoring approved domestic chips guarantee Huawei a buyer even where per-chip performance trails Nvidia, which funds the next architecture revision.

**Global implications.** The interesting claim is architectural. Treating the interconnect as a memory fabric lets Huawei compensate for weaker individual chips by linking far more of them, which erodes export controls as a hard ceiling on Chinese frontier training. The CANN software stack still trails CUDA in library coverage and engineering talent, so how quickly that gap closes determines how much the hardware claim is worth.

**Monday morning.** Ask your infrastructure team what supporting a second, non-Nvidia training stack would cost, and whether any supplier or joint-venture partner already runs on Ascend.

## Reading them together

A governance bloc without a technology stack is a communiqué. A technology stack without a governance bloc is a procurement question. Shanghai spent this week presenting both at the same venue, to the same audience of ministers from countries that have joined neither framework. The competition for those undecided states will be settled by whoever makes the bundle easier to say yes to, and right now one side is offering rules, training places and machines in a single conversation.
