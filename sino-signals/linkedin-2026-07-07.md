What happens to the export-control playbook when a food delivery company trains a frontier model with zero Nvidia chips?

On June 30, Meituan released LongCat-2.0, a 1.6-trillion-parameter mixture-of-experts model with a one-million-token context window, pretrained from scratch on more than 30 trillion tokens using only domestic AI chips. Media reports put the cluster at roughly 50,000 accelerators, widely identified as Huawei Ascend 910C. Days later, Meituan open-sourced the weights under the MIT license.

Why China can do this: the model ran anonymously on OpenRouter as "Owl Alpha" for a month and climbed to the global top three by total usage, taking second place in monthly Claude Code calls. That ranking came from real developer workloads, not benchmark submissions. Meituan prices access at 9.9 yuan for 50 million tokens, backed by 26 billion yuan of 2025 R&D spending, and its delivery and rider-dispatch operations generate agent training data at a scale few companies anywhere possess.

The global impact: LongCat-2.0 is the first trillion-parameter model to complete the full pretraining-to-inference loop on Chinese hardware. The South China Morning Post called it the biggest AI model trained on local chips. Together with Zhipu's GLM-5.2, open Chinese models are now advancing on capability and compute independence at the same time.

Monday morning action: retire any planning assumption that chip controls hold Chinese frontier training below the trillion-parameter scale, and have your engineering team benchmark LongCat-2.0 on agentic coding workloads this week. MIT-licensed weights plus 9.9 yuan pricing reset the cost floor for code automation.
