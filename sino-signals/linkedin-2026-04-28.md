When did your robotics vendor last send you operations data from a paying customer's production line, not a staged demo?

On April 15, Shanghai humanoid maker AGIBOT and contract manufacturer Longcheer Technology announced live deployment of AGIBOT G2 humanoids on Longcheer's tablet production line. This is the first paid embodied AI mass-production contract in consumer electronics, anywhere in the world.

The published numbers are concrete. G2 robots run Multimedia Integrated Testing stations at 310 units per hour. Cycle time is 19 to 20 seconds. Operation rate stays above 99 percent across more than 140 hours of continuous run time. AGIBOT plans to scale to 100 G2 units by Q3 2026 and extend deployments into automotive, semiconductor, and energy plants.

Why China can move at this pace. Longcheer is one of the largest contract manufacturers for smartphones and tablets globally, supplying brands at volume. AGIBOT skipped the pilot stage entirely and went directly to a paid customer line. China produced 143,608 industrial robots in the first two months of 2026, up 31.1 percent year over year. The share of industrial enterprises using large models or AI agents climbed from 9.6 percent in 2024 to 47.5 percent in 2025.

What this changes for Western leaders. Figure, 1X, Apptronik, and Tesla Optimus have published staged demos. AGIBOT is publishing operations data. The competitive question shifts from whether humanoids can do precision tasks to what cycle time, success rate, and cost per task each platform delivers in production.

Monday morning, ask your robotics vendor for hourly throughput and uninterrupted operation duration on a real customer line. If you sit on a board, ask whether your manufacturing strategy still assumes humanoids remain three years out. China just compressed that timeline.
