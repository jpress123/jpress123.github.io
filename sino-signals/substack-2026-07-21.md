# Sino-Signals | Tuesday, July 21, 2026, Two Platforms and One Bundling Strategy

China spent this week selling cheaper stacks, and the price is the argument.

Two launches four days apart, in industries that share nothing except a structural move. China Mobile put ten central state-owned enterprises behind a single industrial platform brand. ByteDance opened its video model to any developer at roughly a third of Google's price. Both bundle layers that Western competitors sell separately, and both landed in the week the World AI Conference filled Shanghai.

## China Mobile and Ten State Giants Launch a National Industrial AI Platform

On July 17, at the World AI Conference enterprise forum, China Mobile launched a unified industrial internet brand called Mobile Tiangong, joined by China Electronics Technology Group, China Electronics and eight other central SOEs. The company calls it the first AI-native industrial internet platform built by a central state enterprise, combining the Tiangong platform, Mobile Cloud, 5G private networks, satellite internet and industrial optical networking into one purchasable stack. The platform already serves more than 300,000 Chinese enterprises, carries over 20 million public-cloud device connections, and holds more than 30,000 deployed 5G plus industrial projects. Named deployments carry hard numbers. At Ansteel, safety administrative work fell 90 percent and unplanned downtime fell 60 percent. At PetroChina, an intelligence layer of roughly one hundred applications raised production stability 16.7 percent and overall efficiency 30 percent.

Ten central SOEs sharing one brand means procurement, standards and data governance move together across steel, energy, shipping and electronics at once. China Mobile owns the network, the compute and the cloud, so a factory buys connectivity and AI decisioning on one contract. State ownership on both sides of the transaction removes the commercial friction that slows industrial software adoption in fragmented Western markets.

Siemens, PTC, Rockwell and Honeywell now compete against a vendor that folds the network layer into the software price. At COSCO Shipping ports, China Mobile ran resident engineering teams through more than ten rounds of retrofit, a service model Western vendors bill as consulting.

Monday morning: add up the last three quotes your operations team received for industrial IoT connectivity, MES and predictive maintenance, then ask your CIO what one bundled contract would have cost instead.

## ByteDance Opens Seedance to Any Developer at a Third of Google's Price

On July 16, ByteDance opened public API access to Seedance 2.5 through its BytePlus cloud platform, completing a rollout announced at the Volcano Engine FORCE conference on June 23. One call now returns a continuous 30 second clip with synchronized audio, generated in a single pass with no stitching. The model accepts up to 50 multimodal reference assets, up from 15 in Seedance 2.0, and supports region-level editing that changes one element of a shot while holding the rest fixed. Artificial Analysis tracks Seedance audio-inclusive generation at roughly $9 per minute against about $24 per minute for Google Veo on Vertex AI. ByteDance reported $2 billion in annual recurring revenue for the enterprise platform before public access opened.

China's short drama industry already ships hundreds of AI-assisted episodes a day, supplying a feedback loop at a volume no Western video model has. Distribution through Douyin and Jianying closes it. With Sora offline since April 26, the enterprise market divides among Seedance, Kling and Veo.

Director Jason Zada priced 15 seconds of high-definition footage at roughly $5 and a 90 second concept reel at a few dozen dollars, which resets what previsualization and regional ad variants cost globally. The legal position stays open. Disney, Warner Bros. Studio employees are reportedly using the tool without formal approval, routing scripts and unreleased footage through an API subject to China's National Intelligence Law.

Both signals run the same play in different markets. Where a Western vendor sells software and lets the customer assemble the rest, the Chinese incumbent sells the assembled thing and prices it against the sum of its parts. That works when one company owns the network, the compute and the distribution, which is the condition China Mobile and ByteDance each satisfy in their own domain. The open question is whether Western procurement teams are still comparing line items while their competitors compare stacks.
