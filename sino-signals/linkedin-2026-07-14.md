What happens to your cost advantage when nearly half of a rival manufacturing base runs AI agents on the factory floor?

That is now the reality in China. IDC data cited by 36Kr and compiled in a July 7 Tianxia Gongchang report shows the share of Chinese industrial enterprises using large models and AI agents jumped from 9.6 percent in 2024 to 47.5 percent in 2025. Firms deploying them across several functions, including R&D, manufacturing, and supply chain, rose from 1.7 percent to 35 percent. At Baosteel's hot-rolling line, Huawei's Pangu model lifted finishing-mill prediction accuracy more than 5 percent and yield by 0.5 percent, which Huawei Cloud values at over 90 million yuan a year.

Why it scales in China: Beijing turned adoption into an execution program. The January 2026 eight-ministry AI Plus Manufacturing plan sets a 2027 target of 1,000 industrial AI agents and 100 industrial datasets, and hands computing-power and model vouchers to smaller factories. A dense supplier base and 4.8 million active factories give any working agent a huge installed base.

Global impact: a base where half of firms run agents can compress cost and quality gaps that took Western plants decades to build. IDC projects Chinese industrial AI spending near 90 billion yuan by 2028. The catch is monetization. McKinsey finds only 6 percent of adopters capture real enterprise value, so scale alone does not settle the productivity lead.

Monday morning: benchmark your quality-inspection and scheduling workflows against Chinese lighthouse-factory results like a 41 percent defect-rate cut. Buy proven agents instead of building in-house, since MIT finds purchased solutions succeed about three times as often, and fund the process redesign that actually moves profit.
