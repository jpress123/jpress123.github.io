What changes when the world's second AI superpower commits, in writing, to one comprehensive law instead of a patchwork?

China's State Council issued its 2026 legislative work plan last week and, for the first time, committed to accelerate comprehensive legislation for the sound development of AI. The text names six pillars, data, computing power, algorithms, property rights, cybersecurity, and supply chains, plus a separate clause covering the regulation of major application scenarios. The National People's Congress simultaneously listed AI legislation as a review item for the third consecutive year. Last year's plan used a single line about promoting AI legislative work, this year's text is the most detailed pronouncement Chinese authorities have ever issued on a planned national AI statute.

China can move from sector-specific rules to one umbrella law because the existing stack already does the work. More than ten regulations, generative AI measures, deep synthesis rules, recommendation-algorithm rules, the May 8 implementation opinions on intelligent agents, and the July 15 anthropomorphic-interaction measures, give the State Council a tested working library to consolidate. Local laws in Hangzhou, Beijing, and Shenzhen serve as legislative laboratories. The Cyberspace Administration filing system has registered hundreds of generative AI services since 2023, producing operational evidence to anchor a statute around.

For Western leaders, this means China would become the second major jurisdiction after the EU with a comprehensive horizontal AI law, while the United States continues with sector-specific executive actions and no federal statute. Multinationals already compliant with the EU AI Act will face a second comprehensive regime with overlapping but non-identical risk classifications. The named focus on AI property rights and supply chain security signals that the law will reach training-data ownership, model-weight provenance, and chip-to-model documentation, areas where US and EU law remain unsettled.

Monday morning: if you run AI compliance, commission a side-by-side mapping of your EU AI Act obligations against the six named pillars and identify the gaps before the draft text circulates. If you train on Chinese-language content, draft a position on AI training-data ownership now, since the property-rights provisions will move first.
