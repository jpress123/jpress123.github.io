What happens to your AI companion product when the world's largest market writes the rulebook first?

On April 10, China's Cyberspace Administration and four other ministries issued Order No. 21, the Interim Measures for the Administration of AI Anthropomorphic Interaction Services. It takes effect July 15. The rules cover any service that uses AI to simulate a real person and provide ongoing emotional interaction: companionship, care, support. Providers cannot design products to induce emotional dependence or addiction, cannot manipulate users into harmful decisions, and must detect users in crisis, intervene, and contact a guardian or emergency contact. Virtual-partner and virtual-kin relationships for minors are banned outright. A minor mode is mandatory. AI interactions must be labeled, and users must be warned after every two hours of continuous use.

Why this matters in China: a single national rule, enforced through the app stores, forces every provider to comply at once. The Cyberspace Administration can demand security assessments from any service above one million registered or 100,000 monthly active users, require algorithm filings, and order non-compliant apps removed. Compliant firms get to scale with regulatory cover instead of legal uncertainty.

Global impact: this is the first binding national framework written specifically for AI companionship, and it lands while Western regulators are still debating the same harms after high-profile cases involving chatbots and minors. Companies selling AI friends, companion apps, and digital humans now have a concrete template covering crisis intervention, minor protection, addiction limits, and AI labeling.

Monday morning: if you ship any companion or emotional-AI feature, map your design against the rules on dependence, minors, and crisis response before July 15. Build crisis detection and human handoff now. These are becoming a baseline expectation, not a differentiator.
