## Four-Department Action Plan Aligns AI Compute With China's Dual Carbon Targets

On May 8, 2026, the NDRC, the National Energy Administration, the MIIT and the National Data Administration jointly released the Action Plan to Promote Bidirectional Empowerment of AI and Energy (国能发科技〔2026〕34号). The plan deploys 29 priority tasks across clean-energy supply to AI data centres, compute-power coordination, AI applications across the full energy chain, energy data-sharing mechanisms and energy-domain model innovation. By 2027 the secure, green and economical energy system supporting AI is to be initially built. By 2030 the plan targets world-leading clean-energy supply for AI compute and world-leading AI applications across energy operations.

China can scale this because data-centre and AI-manufacturing electricity already represents more than 10 percent of newly added power demand, and the four-department mandate aligns NDRC project approval, NEA grid planning, MIIT industrial standards and the National Data Administration's data-sharing rules around one bidirectional target. The Dual Control transition from energy-consumption caps to carbon-emission caps gives every provincial grid a measurable AI-compute carbon ledger.

Western AI infrastructure is still procured by individual hyperscalers on opportunistic power-purchase agreements, while China now anchors a state-coordinated green-AI substrate with binding 2027 and 2030 milestones. The plan establishes a global reference point for measuring AI compute carbon intensity, and Chinese low-carbon AI exports gain certification advantage in EU and ASEAN markets that price embedded carbon.

## MIIT Launches China's First Full-Lifecycle Management Platform for Humanoid Robots

On May 25, 2026, China activated its first full-lifecycle management service platform for humanoid robots in Beijing, led by the MIIT Standardization Technical Committee for Humanoid Robots and Embodied Intelligence. Every humanoid robot leaving the factory now receives a unique digital identity code, with end-to-end tracking across R and D, manufacturing, market access, sales, operation and end-of-life recycling. The platform ensures product traceability, strengthens supply-chain supervision, and clarifies accountability.

China can scale this because domestic manufacturers account for roughly 87 to 90 percent of the more than 13,000 humanoid robots shipped globally in 2025, and the lifecycle platform gives every unit a single source of truth that buyers, regulators and insurers can query. Provincial robotics clusters in Beijing E-Town, Shenzhen, Shanghai and Hangzhou inherit one common identifier schema, while the MIIT Standardization Committee writes the standards the platform operationalises.

Western regulators are still drafting humanoid-robot accountability frameworks, while China now operates a working cradle-to-grave registry that anchors safety, insurance underwriting and product-recall workflows. Exporters competing with Chinese humanoid manufacturers will be asked by EU and US buyers to match the traceability baseline, and the platform pre-positions Chinese standards for IEC and ISO humanoid-robotics working groups.
