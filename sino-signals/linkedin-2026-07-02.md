What happens to the AI race when clean electricity, not chips, becomes the binding constraint?

China just switched on its first AI data center running on 100 percent green power. The facility opened June 26 in Zhongwei, Ningxia, built by China Telecom to a wind powered liquid cooling standard with a direct line to nearby wind and solar. It hits a Power Usage Effectiveness of 1.15, far below the industry norm, on fully renewable supply. It sits inside the Eastern Data, Western Computing plan that moves compute to the renewable rich west.

Why this matters in China: Zhongwei already runs 10 data center parks with roughly 3.31 million server racks and six of the country's top 10 computing providers. Gobi wind and solar let operators sign direct power deals that cut carbon and cost at once. National policy wants renewables covering four fifths of AI data center power by 2030, up from about 11 percent in 2023.

Global impact for Western leaders: clean electricity is the tightest constraint on the AI buildout, and Western operators face grid queues and rising power prices while a 1.15 PUE on renewable supply resets the low carbon benchmark. Chinese cloud and model firms gain a cheaper, lower emission compute base as scrutiny of AI energy use grows.

Monday morning: ask where your AI compute draws its power, and whether direct renewable supply can match a 1.15 PUE before you site the next training cluster on a congested grid.
