What happens to your AI stack when the country supplying your cheapest models decides they are too valuable to export?

On July 7, Reuters reported that China's Ministry of Commerce spent the past month meeting Alibaba, ByteDance, and Zhipu about restricting overseas access to the country's most advanced AI models, including systems not yet released and versions shipped as open weights. Officials weighed classifying the leak or theft of proprietary models as a national security crime and capping which foreign investors can fund domestic AI startups. A May legal roundtable, summarized in a Supreme People's Court journal, already sketched a three-tier plan that keeps frontier models at home while routine open-source tools need only registration.

Why this matters in China: Beijing can attach export licenses to model weights through the same Ministry of Commerce regime it uses for rare earths and chip tools, with no new law required. Since DeepSeek R1, the Qwen, GLM, and DeepSeek families have won global developer share on price and openness. That reach is now leverage the state can meter.

Global impact: Every team routing production traffic through Qwen, Doubao, or GLM APIs faces higher costs and supply risk if frontier access moves domestic-only. It mirrors the US suspension of Anthropic's Fable 5 and Mythos 5 in June, and frontier AI is becoming export-controlled technology in both directions. Weights already downloaded stay yours, so the fight is over the next generation, not today's checkpoints.

Monday morning: Map every dependency on Qwen, GLM, or DeepSeek. Mirror the open weights you rely on today. Stand up a fallback route to a US or European provider before any geo-restriction reaches the terms of service.
