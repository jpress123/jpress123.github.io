What happens to AI economics when one company owns the chip, the server, the model, and the agent that runs on top of all three?

On May 20 at the Alibaba Cloud Summit in Hangzhou, Alibaba launched Qwen3.7-Max, a large language model built as a foundation for AI agents, alongside the in-house Zhenwu M890 AI accelerator from subsidiary T-Head and the rack-scale Panjiu AL128 server. Alibaba says Qwen3.7-Max runs autonomously for up to 35 hours and manages more than 1,000 tool calls without performance degradation. The M890 carries 144 GB of on-chip memory and 800 GB per second of inter-chip bandwidth, roughly three times the prior 810E, and the Panjiu AL128 packs 128 accelerators into a single rack.

Why this matters in China. Alibaba Cloud executive Liu Weiguang framed the company as the only firm in China running all five layers of the AI stack, from chips to agentic applications. That vertical control lets Alibaba tune silicon, server, and model together and price inference for a domestic market where Chinese model token usage already exceeds United States volume. State data-centre demand and the national AI Plus action give the M890 a captive customer base.

Global impact. A Chinese hyperscaler shipping its own training-grade accelerator narrows the single-supplier dependency that has defined global AI infrastructure. If the M890 performs near its claimed specs, Alibaba can price agent workloads against domestic silicon, pressuring cloud margins in markets where Western providers still pay Nvidia list prices.
