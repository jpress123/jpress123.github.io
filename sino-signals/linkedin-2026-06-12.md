What if China could export AI without exporting a single chip or a single byte of its own data?

A coastal city just proved it can. Shantou, Guangdong has built China's first compliant channel for selling AI inference across borders. Overseas requests arrive through submarine cables, run on domestic models like DeepSeek and Qwen inside a physically isolated compute zone, and return abroad with per-token billing. Daily volume jumped from 100 million tokens in late April to over 10 billion by late May. The first commercial loop closed on May 18, when a Singapore user's AI toy called 100,000 tokens billed at 2 yuan per million.

Why this works in China: Shantou lands over half of the country's submarine cable export bandwidth, with 32.7 milliseconds of latency to Singapore. Offshore wind feeds the data centers, so one 0.5-yuan kilowatt-hour of electricity becomes 11 yuan of exported token value, a 22-fold uplift. A state-approved "data processing trade" pilot solves compliance, and 30 percent compute subsidies sweeten the economics. The Chenghai toy cluster, China's toy capital, is already converting itself into an AI toy export base.

The global stakes: this model routes around both chip export controls and data-sovereignty objections. The chips never leave China. The data never enters China's domestic internet. The service crosses the border at one-third to one-half the price of international platforms, with reported customer retention above 70 percent. Southeast Asia gets frontier-adjacent AI at commodity prices, and US hyperscalers get undercut in their fastest-growing market.

Monday morning: if you make consumer devices, price a Chinese token-export pipeline against your current US cloud inference bill. A 2-to-3x cost gap on the AI inside a 20-dollar toy decides who wins the shelf. And watch whether other Chinese port cities replicate the pilot.
