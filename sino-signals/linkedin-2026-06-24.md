What happens to your production budget when a single person can ship a full drama series in a day?

In China, AI generated micro dramas, called manju, are on track to reach a 24 billion yuan market in 2026, with the audience projected to grow 1.3 times to 280 million viewers. More than 1,000 AI short dramas now ship every day. During the 2026 Spring Festival season, AI animated titles drew nearly 30 percent of 8.67 billion total views, with hyper realistic AI clips contributing over 80 percent of that share. ByteDance's Seedance 2.0, Kuaishou Kling, and Shengshu Vidu drove the surge.

Why this scales in China: Douyin, Kuaishou, Tencent, and Baidu launched dedicated manju apps and creator incentives between November and February, giving AI dramas instant distribution to hundreds of millions of daily users. AI production shifts spend from 80 percent labor to 20 percent, with compute and tokens becoming the main cost. A domestic stack of video models, platforms, and payment rails carries a title from script to monetization inside one ecosystem.

Why Western leaders should care: Generative video has reached a mass paying audience at industrial scale, a milestone Hollywood and Western streamers have touched only in pilots. The economics reset what short form video costs to make, pressuring studios, advertising agencies, and game cinematics teams worldwide. China's manju factories now export the template through apps like TikTok and ReelShort.

Monday morning: Commission a small AI native short series this quarter to learn the production economics firsthand. Test AI video pipelines against your current vendor costs before the next budget cycle.
