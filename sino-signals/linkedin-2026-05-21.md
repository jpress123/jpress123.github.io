What happens when a major economy decides AI agents need their own rulebook, separate from the chatbots they grew out of?

On May 8, China answered. The Cyberspace Administration of China, the National Development and Reform Commission, and the Ministry of Industry and Information Technology jointly issued the Implementation Opinions on the Standardized Application and Innovative Development of Intelligent Agents. It is the country's first national policy that treats AI agents as a distinct category, defining them as systems with autonomous perception, memory, decision-making, interaction, and execution. The document sets four areas of measures and names 19 priority deployment scenarios, from scientific research and manufacturing to healthcare, education, judicial services, and public security.

Why this matters in China: the framework routes agent deployment through the same three ministries that already license generative AI and industrial software. Agents in sensitive sectors face filing, mandatory testing, and recall obligations. Naming 19 scenarios hands provincial governments and state enterprises a procurement checklist, which converts policy into deployment inside one budget cycle.

Global impact: China is the first major economy to govern agentic AI as digital infrastructure instead of as an application layer. Its vendors now have a defined compliance path while United States and European Union rule-making still treats agents under general AI statutes. The 19-scenario list doubles as an industrial roadmap, signaling where Chinese agent demand will concentrate through the 15th Five-Year Plan.

Monday morning: map your agent roadmap against China's 19 named scenarios to see where Chinese competitors get policy and procurement support first. If you operate in a regulated sector, brief your legal team on the filing and testing obligations before deploying an agent in any China-facing product.
