## AI Micro Dramas Become World's First Mass Market for Generated Video

China's AI generated micro dramas, called manju, are on track to reach a 24 billion yuan market in 2026, with the audience projected to grow 1.3 times to 280 million viewers, according to mainland data reported by The Standard. More than 1,000 AI short dramas now ship every day. During the 2026 Spring Festival season, AI animated titles drew nearly 30 percent of 8.67 billion total views, with hyper realistic AI clips contributing over 80 percent of that share. ByteDance's Seedance 2.0, Kuaishou Kling, and Shengshu Vidu drove the surge in output.

Why China can scale this: Douyin, Kuaishou, Tencent, and Baidu launched dedicated manju apps and creator incentives between November and February, giving AI dramas instant distribution to hundreds of millions of daily users. AI production shifts spend from 80 percent labor to 20 percent, with compute and tokens becoming the core expense and a one person, one drama a day pace within reach.

Global implications: Generative video has reached a mass paying audience at industrial scale, a milestone Hollywood and Western streamers have touched only in pilots. The economics reset what short form video costs to make, pressuring studios, advertising agencies, and game cinematics teams worldwide, and the template exports through apps like TikTok and ReelShort.

Monday morning: Commission a small AI native short series this quarter to learn the production economics firsthand, and test AI video pipelines against your current vendor costs before the next budget cycle.

## China Orders 10,000 Humanoid Robots Into Real Jobs by Year End

On June 9, 2026, the Ministry of Industry and Information Technology and the State owned Assets Supervision and Administration Commission issued a document ordering local governments and state owned enterprises to test and deploy humanoid robots in real production and service settings within six months. Implementation plans are due by the end of June and progress reports by the end of November. Beijing set a target of more than 100 high value applications and deployment of 10,000 units by year end, spanning manufacturing, warehousing, healthcare, and disaster relief.

Why China can scale this: State ownership lets Beijing convert factories, warehouses, and hospitals into guaranteed deployment sites, giving makers like UBTech, AgiBot, and Unitree a captive demand base. UBTech delivered more than 500 Walker S2 units in 2025 and targets 10,000 in 2026, while AgiBot shipped over 5,100 robots last year, so domestic supply can meet the order.

Global implications: China is using industrial policy to push humanoid robots past the demo stage into measured factory work, the threshold Western firms like Tesla and Figure are still chasing. A coordinated buying program at this scale hands Chinese makers the deployment data and unit volumes that drive down cost faster than rivals, and Western manufacturers may meet Chinese humanoids with proven production records before their own units leave pilot lines.
