DeepSeek just abandoned NVIDIA entirely. What does that mean for your AI strategy?

DeepSeek's next flagship model, V4, launches this week running entirely on Huawei's Ascend 950PR chip with zero NVIDIA dependency. The model uses a Mixture of Experts architecture with one trillion total parameters, activating 37 billion per inference, and supports multimodal input across text, image, and code. DeepSeek delayed its original February release to collaborate with Huawei and Cambricon on substantial code rewrites, rebuilding from CUDA to Huawei's CANN framework.

This is not a proof-of-concept demonstration. V4 is a frontier model from the lab that produced R1 and V3, choosing to ship its flagship product on Chinese silicon. DeepSeek's decision validates Huawei Ascend as a training-grade platform for trillion-parameter models, a threshold no other domestic chip had crossed with a top-tier model maker. China's domestic intelligent computing is projected to exceed 35% of total national computing infrastructure in 2026.

The strategic calculus on semiconductor export controls changes fundamentally. The logic of restricting NVIDIA H100 and A100 access assumed China could not train frontier models on domestic hardware. V4 on Ascend 950PR demonstrates a validated non-CUDA path for trillion-parameter training. NVIDIA's addressable market in China is now shrinking structurally, not just from regulation but from a competing ecosystem that top Chinese labs actively prefer.

If you lead AI infrastructure strategy, model what a two-track chip supply chain means for your procurement over the next three years. If you build on open-source models, prepare for a future where leading Chinese models are optimized for CANN first and CUDA second.
