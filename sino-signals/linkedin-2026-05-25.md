What happens to an entire creative industry when 470 new productions can be spun up every single day, with no actors and no cameras?

China's short drama studios are now releasing an average of 470 fully AI-generated episodes per day, according to research firm DataEye's January 2026 count. The platform FlexTV has stopped all traditionally shot productions and moved entirely to AI. Kunlun Tech, parent of the apps DramaWave and FreeReels, now lists more than 1,000 AI titles, and rival StoReels targets 100 AI dramas a month. A North American production that once cost roughly 200,000 US dollars now costs 80 to 90 percent less, and the production cycle has fallen from three or four months to under one.

Why this matters in China. The short drama industry hit 6.9 billion US dollars in 2024, passing the country's annual box office for the first time. Platforms already pick content by performance data, not creative instinct, so generative AI slots straight into the pipeline. Domestic video models from ByteDance (Seedance) and Kuaishou (Kling) give Chinese studios a low-cost generation stack that foreign rivals lack.

Global impact. The worldwide microdrama market reached 11 billion US dollars in 2025, and Omdia projects 14 billion by the end of 2026. The United States supplies about half of overseas revenue. Chinese studios of roughly 10 people are producing English-language series for US audiences, making short drama China's first mass cultural export built on generative video.

Monday morning. If you run a media business, commission one test AI series and benchmark its cost per minute and completion rate against your live-action output. Expect creative roles to shift toward AI asset curators who turn scripts into model prompts, and plan reskilling now.
