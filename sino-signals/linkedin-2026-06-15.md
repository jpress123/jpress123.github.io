What happens to your AI companion product when the world's largest market makes "don't engineer emotional dependency" a legal requirement with named fines?

China just answered. On April 10 the Cyberspace Administration of China and four ministries issued Order No. 21, the Interim Measures for the Management of AI Anthropomorphic Interaction Services. It is the first binding national rule for AI companion and emotional-support apps, and it takes effect July 15, 2026. The rule covers any service that simulates a person's personality, thinking, and communication style for continuous emotional interaction. Providers must run a minor mode, block virtual-partner and virtual-kin services for minors, intervene when a user signals self-harm by contacting guardians or emergency contacts, and remind users of continuous use every two hours.

Why this lands so fast in China: the same algorithm-filing regime that already governs every recommendation engine in the country absorbs the new rule. Any companion app with 1 million registered users or 100,000 monthly actives must file a security assessment with provincial authorities. App stores must verify compliance before listing. Fines reach 200,000 yuan where a user's life or health is harmed.

For Western leaders, the timing is the story. US courts are still litigating whether chatbot makers owe a duty of care after teen-suicide cases tied to Character.AI. China has converted those contested design questions, emotional dependency, sycophancy, minor protection, into legal obligations with penalties. Regulators drafting child-safety and companion rules now have a working template, and a competitor who moved first.

Monday morning: if you ship a companion, character, or emotional-support feature, map it against these measures this week. The China access date is July 15, and the two-hour warning, self-harm intervention, and minor-mode mandates are becoming the global floor, not a China-only cost.
