What happens to your AI compliance program when the world stops agreeing on one rulebook?

On July 16, twenty-nine countries signed the founding agreement for the World Artificial Intelligence Cooperation Organization. WAICO is headquartered permanently in Shanghai. Founding members include Russia, Indonesia, Brazil, Pakistan, Kazakhstan and Laos. No major Western democracy signed. The next morning Xi Jinping opened the World AI Conference with his first keynote in the event's nine-year history, pledged 5,000 AI training places for people from developing countries over five years, and announced AI application cooperation centers with ASEAN, the Arab League, the African Union and CELAC. UN Secretary-General Antonio Guterres attended the signing ceremony without joining the body.

Why this works in China: Premier Li Qiang floated the organization at WAIC 2025 and Beijing spent a year turning a proposal into signatures. The offer pairs institutional membership with things member states actually want and currently lack, namely open-weight models, training pipelines and affordable compute. Hosting the secretariat in Shanghai gives Chinese ministries permanent agenda-setting access.

Why Western leaders should care: there are now two institutional frameworks for AI governance, one anchored in Brussels, Washington and the G7, the other assembling in Shanghai. Countries that align to WAICO can write AI rules that owe nothing to the EU AI Act, the OECD AI Principles or the Hiroshima Process. Governance institutions, once founded, tend to persist. Most of the world's countries have joined neither bloc, and that is the population both sides are now competing for.

Monday morning: ask your general counsel which of your growth markets signed the WAICO charter, and whether your AI compliance program quietly assumes one global standard. Where it does, scope the cost of a second compliance track now, before the first member state publishes a binding rule.
