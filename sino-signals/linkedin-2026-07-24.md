While Washington and Brussels argue over export controls, did you notice China just built the table everyone else will have to sit at?

On July 16, twenty-nine countries signed the founding agreement for the World Artificial Intelligence Cooperation Organization, a new intergovernmental body headquartered in Shanghai. Xi Jinping announced it the next day at the World AI Conference. Founding members include Russia, Pakistan, Indonesia, Brazil, and Kazakhstan, with UN Secretary-General Antonio Guterres at the opening. China paired the launch with 5,000 AI training slots for developing countries over five years and an offer to share its MAZU weather-warning system with 30 nations.

Why it matters in China: Beijing has hosted WAIC every year since 2018, and now it anchors a permanent governance body on home soil. Its open-source models have been downloaded more than 10 billion times. That gives WAICO real infrastructure to hand members, not just communiqués. Models, training, and standards become a durable bloc.

Global impact: WAICO stands up a China-centered alternative to Western-led AI governance forums exactly as the US and EU tighten controls and diverge on rules. For any country weighing whose standards and models to adopt, a Shanghai body offering free tools, training, and open weights is a concrete pull. Western governments now face a competing standards track that could set defaults across the Global South.

Monday morning: if you operate across Asia, Africa, or Latin America, map which of your markets are joining WAICO and whether Chinese standards will shape local procurement. Brief policy and compliance on a two-track governance world this week.
