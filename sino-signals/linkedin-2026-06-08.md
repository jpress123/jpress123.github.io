What happens when a government decides to regulate a feeling?

On July 15, China's Interim Measures for AI Anthropomorphic Interactive Services take effect. The Cyberspace Administration of China, joined by four other agencies, finalized rules covering virtual companions, emotional chatbots, and human-like assistants that simulate a real person and sustain ongoing emotional interaction. Providers must obtain guardian consent for users under 14, give parents controls over time, spending, and which characters a child can reach, and offer an easy exit that stops the service the moment a user asks.

Why this matters in China: the AI companion market already counts tens of millions of users across apps like MiniMax's Talkie and Xingye, plus Baidu and Tencent. One national rule, enforced through the CAC's existing filing and takedown system, sets product terms across every provider at once. The labeling and registration plumbing was already built for earlier generative AI rules, so platforms can comply fast.

Global impact: this is one of the first dedicated national frameworks for AI companionship and emotional dependency. EU and US lawmakers now have a working regulatory template to study. Western apps such as Character.AI and Replika that reach Chinese users face direct compliance obligations, and the exit-on-request and guardian-control mechanics could quickly become global product norms within a year.

Monday morning: audit any consumer AI that simulates personality or builds emotional rapport. Look for minor-safety gaps, exit friction, and weak disclosure before regulators at home write the same rules. Treat China's guardian controls and stop-on-request requirement as a design checklist, not a foreign edge case.
