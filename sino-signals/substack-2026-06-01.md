## State Grid Commits 6.8 Billion Yuan to 8,500 Robots, the Largest Embodied-Robot Order on Record

On April 24, 2026, State Grid Corporation of China issued its 2026 Embodied Intelligence Development Plan, committing about 6.8 billion yuan (about 1 billion US dollars) to procure roughly 8,500 robots this year. Hardware takes 5.8 billion yuan across three classes: 5,000 quadruped inspection robot dogs (1.5 billion yuan) for substations and transmission lines, 500 humanoid live-line work robots (2.5 billion yuan) for distribution and ultra-high-voltage work, and 3,000 dual-arm inspection units (1.8 billion yuan). The remaining 1 billion yuan funds R&D (800 million) and talent (200 million). Body suppliers include Unitree, UBTech, Zhipu, Deep Robotics, and Fourier.

China can scale this because the buyer is enormous and disciplined. State Grid runs an annual intelligence budget near 80 billion yuan, so this order is 8.75 percent of one year's spend, and procurement runs in three phases across 2026. Vendors must meet State Grid's Power Embodied Intelligent Equipment specification and adapt to its Guangming power model with local deployment, which standardizes the whole supplier field around one state buyer.

The global implication is a cost curve. One buyer ordering 8,500 units in a year sets unit-cost and reliability benchmarks no Western utility approaches, and pulls Unitree, UBTech, and Fourier up a volume curve that lowers their export prices. State Grid projects 500,000 to 800,000 yuan in annual labor savings per unit, a 2 to 3 year payback, five times faster inspection, and an 80 percent cut in safety incidents.

Monday morning, if you run critical infrastructure, price a pilot of quadruped or dual-arm inspection robots against your current manual inspection cost and safety record, and ask whether your robotics suppliers can match a 2 to 3 year payback at fleet scale.

## TC260 Issues the Ethics-Safety Guidelines for AI Applications 1.0

On May 19, 2026, China's National Cybersecurity Standardization Technical Committee (TC260) issued TC260-005, the Ethics-Safety Guidelines for AI Applications 1.0. The drafting roster is the tell: Tsinghua University, the China Electronics Standardization Institute, Shanghai Jiao Tong University, Alibaba, Huawei, and DeepSeek, with Tsinghua's Xue Lan among the lead authors. The document sets nine ethics-safety principles and concrete guidance for developers, providers, and users, including a rule that users can always refuse, interrupt, or shut down a service and that providers avoid systems users cannot stop except by cutting power.

China can scale this because TC260 documents work as the de facto compliance baseline that binding rules cite. The guideline propagates through the May agent-governance framework and the personified-interaction measure that takes effect July 15, and with Alibaba, Huawei, and DeepSeek in the room, the largest vendors are pre-aligned to one ethics floor.

Globally, the guideline writes down requirements that Western voluntary frameworks still debate: human override at key nodes, loss-of-control risk review before shipping highly autonomous agents, and assist-only AI in national-security, public-service, and life-and-death decisions. It carves specific duties for finance and for minors, elderly, and disabled users.

Monday morning, pull TC260-005 and test your highest-autonomy product against the human-override and stop-mechanism clauses, and confirm your system stays assist-only where the guideline requires it.
