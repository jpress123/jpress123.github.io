## CATL's Battery Swap Network Passes 2,000 Stations Across China

CATL announced on July 1, 2026 that its Choco-SEB battery swap network reached 2,000 operational stations across China as of June 30. The network spans 31 provinces and 180 cities, with over 80 percent coverage in small and medium cities, and it added more than 200 stations a month through the second quarter, up from 700 stations in late 2025. CATL has opened its first two stations in Hong Kong and targets 3,000 stations across more than 190 mainland cities by the end of 2026. New sites now ship with Shenxing supercharging piles, so each station both swaps packs and fast charges.

Why China can scale this: CATL supplies close to half of the country's EV batteries, so it controls the cell chemistry, the swap hardware, and the standards at the same time. That vertical grip lets it roll out a national grid faster than any pure charging operator, and its size lets it standardize packs across brands.

Global implications: battery swap turns an EV from a product into an energy service, cutting the sticker price by unbundling the pack and removing charging waits. Drivers can even earn about 40 yuan a day by returning batteries during peak power pricing, which turns parked packs into distributed grid storage that most Western utilities lack. A 2,000-station base pressures global rivals to license CATL's system or watch swap-ready EVs undercut them on upfront cost.

Monday morning: model how a swap network with grid-storage revenue changes total cost of ownership against your own charging bets, then decide whether to license, partner, or compete.

## China's Jiuzhang 4.0 Claims Quantum Advantage Beyond Any Supercomputer

A team led by Chao-Yang Lu and Jian-Wei Pan at the University of Science and Technology of China published Jiuzhang 4.0 in Nature on May 15, 2026. The programmable photonic processor feeds 1,024 squeezed-light inputs into an 8,176-mode circuit and detects up to 3,050 photons in a single run. For its main benchmark the team estimates El Capitan, the world's fastest supercomputer, would need more than 10 to the 42nd power years to reproduce one sample the machine generates in 25.6 microseconds. The paper is peer reviewed and the raw data are public.

Why China can scale this: the work comes from the USTC-Hefei ecosystem, a concentration of talent, cryogenic detectors, and photonics fabrication that no rival lab matches. State funding has run continuously since the first Jiuzhang in 2020, and each generation has widened the photon count by an order of magnitude.

Global implications: Gaussian boson sampling is a narrow task chosen because photons are naturally good at it, so this is not general-purpose or code-breaking quantum computing. The strategic weight sits in the engineering, ultra-stable phase locking and 92 percent source efficiency, the same ingredients that fault-tolerant photonics will need. Western ventures such as PsiQuantum and Xanadu now face a state-funded group running the largest photonic system on record.

Monday morning: brief boards that this is a photonics milestone, not a threat to current encryption, and track which groups steer this hardware toward molecular simulation.
