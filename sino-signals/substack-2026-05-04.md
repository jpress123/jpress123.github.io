## China Five Ministries Issue Order 21 Regulating Personified AI Companions

The Cyberspace Administration of China, NDRC, MIIT, Ministry of Public Security, and State Administration for Market Regulation jointly issued Order No. 21, the Interim Measures on the Administration of AI Personified Interactive Services, effective July 15, 2026. The 32-article rule covers any AI service that simulates a natural person's personality, thinking, or communication style for sustained emotional interaction. Providers cannot offer virtual relatives or virtual companion services to minors. Users under 14 require parental consent. Pop-up alerts must trigger every two hours of continuous use. When a user shows extreme emotional or self-harm signals, providers must intervene and contact guardians or emergency contacts. Services with one million registered users or 100,000 monthly actives must file a safety assessment with the provincial cyberspace administration. Penalties run to 100,000 yuan for non-compliance and 200,000 yuan where harm to user life or health occurs.

China hosts the world's largest companion-AI market, with Xingye, Zhumeng Dao, and Doubao's character mode aggregating tens of millions of monthly users. Joint sign-off across five ministries pulls enforcement out of pure cyberspace remit and into product safety, public security, and industrial policy in one stroke. Application stores must verify safety assessments before listing, so distribution-layer enforcement begins on July 15 alongside platform-side rules.

China is the first major jurisdiction to regulate AI companions as a distinct product category with binding minor-protection, time-use, and emotional-intervention requirements. The EU AI Act treats companion AI under general-purpose risk tiers. The US has no federal equivalent. Western platforms with China exposure, including Character.AI, Replika, and Talkie, face a hard July 15 deadline or de-listing. Order 21 will become a reference text for EU DSA implementing acts and California SB-243 follow-on legislation.

## State Council Document 7 Targets 100 Trillion Yuan Service Sector With AI Procurement Mandate

On April 21, the State Council issued Document Guofa No. 7, twenty articles directing China's service sector to reach 100 trillion yuan in scale by 2030, up from 80.9 trillion yuan in 2025 and 57.7 percent of GDP. The software and information services article explicitly directs central and local government bodies to deepen the AI-Plus action by procuring large models and intelligent agent services and accelerating intelligent programming tool deployment. The document also instructs banks, insurers, and leasing firms to expand supply-chain finance using inventory, orders, and warehouse receipts as collateral, and tells planners to build national AI-application incubation bases.

Document 7 is the first State Council policy that converts AI from a research priority into a public-procurement category, with explicit budget authority for ministries and SOEs to buy commercial models and agents. State-owned banks, the big-three telecoms, State Grid, and provincial governments operate combined annual procurement budgets above 5 trillion yuan. A 1 percent allocation to agent and model procurement creates a 50 billion yuan demand pool. The document pairs with the January 2026 AI-Plus Manufacturing implementation opinion, which set a 2027 target of 1,000 industrial agents and 100 quality industrial datasets.

Western governments still treat large-model procurement as a contested capability ladder, with US OMB M-24-10 and EU AI Act compliance frameworks slowing federal deployment. China is moving in the opposite direction. Domestic vendors including Zhipu, Moonshot, MiniMax, Baichuan, DeepSeek, and Alibaba's Tongyi gain a guaranteed government and SOE demand pool, accelerating unit-economics improvement against US frontier vendors.

## The Stack Effect

Order 21 sets the consumer-safety floor. Document 7 sets the public-sector demand ceiling. Domestic Chinese vendors that clear the first now face guaranteed access to the second. Western vendors lacking either credential will struggle to enter, regardless of model quality. The lesson from the past fortnight: Beijing now treats AI policy as a two-sided problem, and is willing to legislate both sides on the same calendar.
