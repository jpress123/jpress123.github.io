When your engineers reach for a coding model this week, do they know whose law governs the code they paste into it?

The world's developers have already answered the speed-and-price question. OpenRouter, the gateway that about 8 million developers use to route work across 400-plus large language models, disclosed around its May 26 funding round that Chinese open-weight models held roughly 61 percent of token volume across its ten most-used models in late February, and about 51 percent of all platform traffic through April. Coding and agentic workloads now make up more than half of the platform's tokens, and that is where Chinese models concentrate. Moonshot AI's Kimi K2.6 scored 58.6 percent on the SWE-Bench Pro benchmark by its own testing, edging GPT-5.5. MiniMax and DeepSeek price inference near 0.30 US dollars per million input tokens against roughly 5 dollars for Claude Opus.

Why it scales in China: mixture-of-experts designs cut compute per query, and the backers treat cheap APIs as strategy. DeepSeek runs on a hedge fund and says it does not need API revenue. Alibaba's Qwen and Zhipu's GLM sit inside cloud businesses that can absorb loss-leader pricing. Open weights spread without a sales team.

Global impact: a cost gap above ten to one compounds across millions of calls per agent session, pressuring every Western lab that bills per token. Western proprietary systems still hold about 70 percent of total global API spend and dominate regulated finance, healthcare, and government work. China's National Intelligence Law turns the cheap option into a data-governance decision, and a US congressional committee opened an investigation on April 29.

Monday morning: audit which model providers your engineers already route to, and set policy on what code and customer data may touch a Chinese-hosted endpoint. Treat open-weight cost curves as a board-level pricing input, not a developer footnote.
