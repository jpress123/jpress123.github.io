Is your AI product China-ready when the regulator publishes a fourteen-point compliance grid in a single notice?

On April 30, the Cyberspace Administration of China deployed a four-month "Qinglang · Rectification of AI Application Chaos" special action. Two stages, fourteen specific violation categories, with penalties extending to accounts, MCN agencies, and host platforms.

Stage 1 targets the source side: failure to file large models under the Generative AI Services Interim Measures, weak safety review and content filtering, training-corpus security, AI data poisoning via generative search engine optimization, incomplete AIGC labeling under the March 2025 Labeling Measures, misuse of agent and digital-human technology, and lax open-source community governance.

Stage 2 targets the content side: AI-generated digital slop (数字泔水), deepfake impersonation of public figures, AI-modified classics, violent and pornographic synthetic content, harm to minors via animation re-creations, AI-managed network water army account farms, and unlicensed AI products including stripping tools and AI fortune-telling.

This pairs with revised Cybersecurity Law penalties effective January 1, 2026 that lift maximum entity fines to 10 million yuan, the April 4 Online Livestream Gifting Notice, and the April 10 Online Platform Pricing Rules. The Cyberspace Administration, the State Administration for Market Regulation, and the National Development and Reform Commission now run a coordinated enforcement perimeter across recommendation, monetization, and AI generation.

Western implication: cloud and model providers selling into multinational enterprises with China operations will see procurement teams ask for the equivalent of a Qinglang readiness statement before contracts close. The EU AI Act implementation will be benchmarked against the operational specificity of this two-stage model.
