# Sino-Signals | July 6, 2026 — The Regulator Writes the Manual, the Market Writes the Check

*China's financial watchdog issues a 32-point AI rulebook for every bank and insurer, while Kuaishou's Kling closes the largest video-AI raise in history.*

Two signals today show China's AI buildout advancing on parallel tracks. The state is codifying how regulated industries must govern AI, and private capital is concentrating behind the companies already monetizing it. Both moves set reference points Western leaders will be measured against.

## China's Financial Regulator Hands Banks a 32-Point AI Rulebook

The National Financial Regulatory Administration issued the Guiding Opinions on Safe AI Development and Application in Banking and Insurance on June 18, 2026, document Jin Fa 2026 No. 8. The framework sets 32 requirements across seven areas: governance architecture, model development and testing, data governance, compute buildout, risk management, capability building, and supervision. Institutions carry accountability under a whoever-uses-it-is-responsible principle, high-risk applications require human oversight and intervention at key stages, and outsourcing and supply-chain risk fall inside the mandate.

Why China can scale this: the rules bind every bank and insurer in the world's largest banking system at once, converting the sector into a single testbed for classification-based AI risk management. Large institutions are told to export compute services to smaller peers and co-build shared infrastructure, so compliance doubles as sector-wide capability building, and the State Council's AI Plus action opinion puts central weight behind enforcement.

Globally, this is among the first comprehensive AI rulebooks from a major national financial regulator, arriving while US and EU supervisors still lean on general-purpose law and scattered guidance. Foreign banks in China must comply, and regulators elsewhere gain a template pairing risk tiers with mandatory human-override points.

Monday morning: chief risk officers should map the 32 requirements against their AI inventories, and China-facing compliance teams should start gap assessments now. The document is in force.

## Kuaishou's Kling Raises $3 Billion, a Record for Video AI

Kuaishou disclosed through a Hong Kong exchange filing on July 2 that its Beijing Kling unit completed an external raise of up to 3 billion dollars at a post-money valuation near 18 billion, the largest funding round for any video-generation company. CPE, Guofang Venture Capital, BlueFive, Tencent, a Zhongguancun Science City fund, and CITIC Securities co-led, with Alibaba Cloud, Baidu, Huace Film and TV, and Mango-affiliated investors joining. Kling passed 100 million global users in June, serves nearly 50,000 enterprise clients, and hit 500 million dollars in annualized revenue in March.

Why China can scale this: first-quarter revenue passed 650 million yuan, up more than 300 percent year over year, on three paying scenarios, e-commerce video, short drama, and film and television advertising. China's micro-drama industry supplies industrial demand for batch video generation that consumer markets elsewhere lack, and backing from Tencent, Alibaba Cloud, and Baidu wires Kling into compute and distribution at once.

Globally, an 18 billion dollar valuation on real enterprise revenue shows video generation monetizing at industrial scale while Western rivals still search for durable business models. Analysts expect the raise to squeeze smaller video-AI startups on compute, capital, and clients, and investors hold a buyback right if Kling misses an IPO by October 30, 2031.

Monday morning: media and advertising executives should price Kling's enterprise API against current production costs for commerce video and episodic content.
