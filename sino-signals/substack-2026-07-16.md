China is scaling two fronts of physical AI at once: machines that build things and the clean power that runs them.

Two signals this morning show China pushing artificial intelligence out of the data center and into the physical economy. Humanoid robots are now doing precision work on real assembly lines, while a new national plan wires AI directly into the country's energy and carbon strategy. Together they mark a shift from AI as software to AI as industrial infrastructure.

## AgiBot Puts Humanoid Robots to Work on Chinese Assembly Lines

In Nanchang, AgiBot's Genie G2 humanoid robots have worked full shifts on a live consumer-electronics line, running final quality inspection at 310 units per hour with a success rate above 99.9 percent, Xinhua reported on April 14, 2026. The Shanghai-built machines calibrate to a new product in about five minutes and switch production lines in under four hours, work that fixed automation cannot do. Tablet maker Longcheer plans to expand its fleet to 100 units by the third quarter of 2026. This is embodied AI on payroll, not a lab demo.

China can scale this because it shipped nearly 80 percent of the world's humanoid robots in 2025, with AgiBot alone holding a 39 percent global share. Unitree is targeting up to 20,000 units in 2026 from a Yangtze Delta super-factory, and a June 2026 joint action by MIIT and SASAC sets targets of more than 100 deployment scenarios and 10,000-unit rollouts. A dense supply base of motors, sensors, and low-cost batteries cuts prices faster than rivals can match.

The global implication is a reset of the labor math for consumer electronics, autos, and semiconductors, the sectors AgiBot names next. Chinese humanoid output is forecast to nearly double in 2026, pressuring Western manufacturers on cost and speed this year. Whoever fields reliable embodied AI at factory scale first captures the data flywheel that trains the next generation.

## China Ties AI Data Centers to Green Power in New Carbon Plan

China's National Energy Administration released the Energy Sector Energy Conservation and Carbon Reduction Action Plan for 2026 to 2028 on July 10, 2026, the first national carbon plan to write artificial intelligence into its core. It directs AI-model innovation across energy production, supply, storage, and use, and pushes AI computing facilities to raise their green-electricity share while exposing computing loads as flexible demand the grid can shift. In transport, it upgrades high-power charging first at stations above 40 percent holiday utilization and scales new-energy heavy trucks and green shipping fuels.

China can scale this through state planning that turns a directive into installed capacity. The country runs the world's largest EV charging network and its fastest data-center buildout, so one plan can steer where hundreds of new AI campuses draw power. Authorities want renewables to supply roughly four-fifths of AI data-center power by 2030, up from about 11 percent in 2023.

The global implication is a template. Chinese data-center power demand is projected to rise 300 billion to 500 billion kilowatt-hours by 2030, close to a fifth of the country's total electricity-demand growth. By making green-power share a metric for AI infrastructure, Beijing gives Western regulators and hyperscalers a model to study as their own data centers strain local grids.
