Who is accountable when an autonomous AI agent acts on your behalf, and have you decided that before it ships?

China just answered first. On May 8, 2026, the Cyberspace Administration of China, the National Development and Reform Commission, and the Ministry of Industry and Information Technology jointly issued the first national guidelines written specifically for AI agents. The rules define an agent as a system that can perceive, remember, decide, interact, and execute on its own. They require that the user keep the final right to be informed and the right to halt the agent. They name 19 priority deployment scenarios across research, industry, consumption, and public services, and they add filing, testing, and product recall measures for sensitive sectors.

Why this matters in China: the AI plus campaign is pushing agents into government services, factories, and consumer apps at once. One central rulebook aligns ministries, platforms, and local governments on the same targets, and naming 19 scenarios tells buyers and vendors where to deploy first. Compliant firms get regulatory cover to scale agents that complete real transactions, not just answer questions.

Global impact for Western leaders: this is the first national framework built for agents, the exact technology US and European firms are racing to ship without settled rules on authority and liability. By fixing a stop control and a decision-authority boundary, China sets an early reference point for accountability when an agent acts alone. Regulators elsewhere now have a concrete template to study or answer.

Monday morning action: define decision-authority limits and a human stop control before you scale any agent, and have legal map liability for autonomous actions now. These are becoming baseline expectations, not differentiators.
