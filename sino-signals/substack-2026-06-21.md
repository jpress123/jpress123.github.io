Sino-Signals | Sunday, June 21, 2026 — China Races Ahead on Agents While Writing the First Rulebook for AI Companionship

China is pushing AI into 1.4 billion daily lives and drawing the guardrails at the same time.

Two signals this week show both halves of that move. Tencent is testing an AI agent inside WeChat that can act, not just answer, across the country's dominant super app. And Beijing has issued the world's first binding national rules for AI companionship, effective July 15. One expands what AI can do inside a platform; the other sets the limits. Read together, they sketch the model China wants for agentic, emotional AI.

## Tencent Tests a WeChat Agent That Runs Tasks Across the Super App

Tencent is testing a prototype AI agent inside WeChat that can move through the app's mini-programs and complete multi-step tasks, such as searching flight schedules, selecting passenger details, paying with WeChat Pay, and returning a confirmation. The Financial Times reported on June 2 that Tencent planned to begin the regulatory compliance process as soon as June and to test the agent with a small group of external users before any public launch. Tencent declined to comment on timing. Its shares rose as much as 8 percent on the report, as the company races Alibaba and ByteDance after launching its Hy3 flagship model in April under former OpenAI researcher Yao Shunyu.

Why China can scale this: WeChat already unifies messaging, payments, shopping, and transport for roughly 1.4 billion users across millions of mini-programs. A native agent inherits identities, payment rails, and merchant services rather than waiting for each service to build its own AI, which lets it execute real transactions, the step most Western assistants still struggle to finish.

Global implications: an agent that turns a super app into an execution layer resets the contest for agentic commerce, where OpenAI is adding services to ChatGPT from the opposite direction. If WeChat completes purchases reliably, it benchmarks the conversion of AI intent into paid transactions that Western platforms lack the integrated payments and merchant base to match quickly.

Monday morning: study how an agent layered on existing payments and merchant rails changes conversion, and plan for agent-mediated discovery, where ranking rules, not search ads, decide which products get chosen.

## China Issues the World's First National Rules for AI Companion Services

On April 10, the Cyberspace Administration of China and four other ministries issued Order No. 21, the Interim Measures for the Administration of AI Anthropomorphic Interaction Services, effective July 15. The rules cover any service that uses AI to simulate a real person and provide ongoing emotional interaction such as companionship, care, or support, while exempting customer service, knowledge Q and A, and education tools. Providers cannot induce emotional dependence or addiction or manipulate users into harmful decisions, and they must detect users in crisis, intervene, and contact a guardian or emergency contact. Virtual-partner and virtual-kin services for minors are banned, a minor mode is mandatory, AI interactions must be labeled, and users must be warned after every two hours of continuous use.

Why China can scale this: a single national rule enforced through app stores compels every provider at once. The Cyberspace Administration can demand security assessments from any service above one million registered or 100,000 monthly active users, require algorithm filings, and order non-compliant apps removed.

Global implications: this is the first binding national framework written specifically for AI companionship, and it arrives as Western regulators debate the same harms after cases involving chatbots and minors. Companies selling AI friends, companion apps, and digital humans now have a concrete compliance template.

Monday morning: map any companion or emotional-AI feature against the rules on dependence, minors, and crisis response before July 15, and build crisis detection and human handoff now.
