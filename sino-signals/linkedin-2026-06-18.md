What happens when a national government, not a venture market, decides where AI meets the power grid first?

China's National Energy Administration just released its first list of 51 high-value AI energy scenarios, alongside the China AI plus Energy Development Report 2026, at a national promotion conference in Guangzhou on May 26. The scenarios span eight domains, including grids, new energy, hydropower, and thermal power, and name concrete targets: smart grid planning, virtual power plants, vehicle-grid interaction, and renewable power forecasting. Energy chief Wang Hongzhi framed the field as moving from pilots to nationwide rollout, and 25 energy firms signed an open-application initiative.

Why this matters in China: the country runs the world's largest power system and has already built 42 ten-thousand-card computing clusters, so it holds both the grid data and the compute to train energy models at national scale. A central regulator naming priority scenarios points utilities, grid operators, and equipment makers at the same targets at once, compressing the path from demo to deployment.

Global impact: this treats grid intelligence as industrial policy, a model most Western markets pursue through scattered private pilots. If China cuts curtailment and balances renewables faster through AI dispatch, it lowers the cost of its clean-energy transition and its AI buildout at the same time. Grid software, forecasting, and virtual-power-plant vendors outside China now face a benchmark set by national mandate.

Monday morning: map these 51 scenarios against your own backlog and find where AI dispatch or forecasting could cut curtailment this year. Treat AI-energy integration as one cost lever for both decarbonization and compute growth, not two separate programs.
