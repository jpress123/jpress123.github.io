What happens to Nvidia's China business when Beijing publishes the official list of which AI chips the government is allowed to buy?

On May 26, China's Information Technology Security Evaluation Center and its National Security Technology Evaluation Center certified nine domestic AI training and inference chips on the "secure and reliable" procurement list. The roster covers Huawei HiSilicon Ascend 310 and 910, Alibaba T-Head Zhenwu M530 and M890, Biren Bilian 166, Hygon DCU-3G, Iluvatar CoreX KCC-V100X, MetaX MXC600, and Moore Threads PH100. It is the first time AI accelerators have entered the framework, each certification runs three years, and Cambricon and Baidu's Kunlunxin are notably absent.

Why it matters in China. This is the Xinchuang mechanism that already purged Intel and AMD CPUs and Oracle databases from sensitive systems, now pointed at AI compute. Central agencies and state-owned enterprises effectively buy from one menu of nine. A chip off the list loses the most protected slice of the market, and state-scale demand drives each approved vendor down the cost curve.

Global impact. Nvidia sits outside the list, deepening its displacement in government and SOE infrastructure even where export rules permit sales. The nine winners gain a guaranteed home base that funds R&D and yield gains, then carry that pricing into Southeast Asia, the Middle East, and Africa. Western chipmakers now face a state-curated roster, not single firms.

Monday morning. Map which of your China revenue touches government or SOE buyers and treat that share as closed to uncertified chips. If you meet Huawei, Biren, or Moore Threads in third markets, price against vendors with a captive domestic base.
