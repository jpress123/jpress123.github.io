## China Launches Global AI Cooperation Organization With Twenty-Nine Founding Nations

On July 16, 2026, twenty-nine countries signed the founding agreement for the World Artificial Intelligence Cooperation Organization (WAICO), an intergovernmental body headquartered in Shanghai. Xi Jinping announced it the next day in his WAIC keynote. Founding members include Russia, Pakistan, Indonesia, Brazil, and Kazakhstan alongside African and Asian states, with UN Secretary-General Antonio Guterres present at the opening. China paired the launch with a pledge of 5,000 AI training opportunities for developing countries over five years and an offer to share its MAZU meteorological warning system with 30 nations.

China can scale this because it has hosted WAIC every year since 2018 and now anchors a permanent governance body on its own soil, giving Beijing a standing convening role over Global South AI policy. Its open-source models, downloaded more than 10 billion times, and its capacity-building programs give the organization real infrastructure to offer members.

The global implication is a China-centered alternative to Western-led governance forums, arriving as the US and EU tighten export controls and diverge on rules. For countries weighing whose standards to adopt, a Shanghai body offering training, tools, and open weights is a concrete draw that could set defaults across much of the Global South.

## Chinese Academy of Sciences Deploys AI Science Model to Accelerate Discovery

The Chinese Academy of Sciences released PanShi Science Foundation Model 2.0 at WAIC on July 17, 2026, one year after the first version. The team assembled 8 million high-quality scientific reasoning records across more than 200 research tasks, training the model to reason along knowledge and evidence chains and to handle understanding, prediction, and generation across scientific modalities. PanShi 2.0 already runs in mechanics, astronomy, and chemistry and has reached more than 30 universities, research institutes, and central state-owned enterprises.

China can scale this because PanShi pairs a general-plus-specialist training path with a compute base that combines supercomputing, intelligent computing, and high-speed scientific computers, letting it generate its own high-precision data for molecular dynamics and quantum chemistry. State funding and a unified national compute network let one CAS platform serve dozens of institutions by direction, not by market adoption.

The global implication is a shared national backbone for AI-driven science, something Western efforts, scattered across private labs and individual universities, currently lack. A state model that standardizes scientific reasoning and feeds its own simulation data shortens the loop from literature review to experiment.
