Sino-Signals | May 25, 2026 — China Commits to Its First Comprehensive AI Law

Beijing just put a unified AI statute on its 2026 legislative calendar, and the regulatory gap with the West is closing faster than most leaders expect.

For years China governed artificial intelligence through a patchwork of rules, each one aimed at a single problem such as recommendation algorithms, deep synthesis, or generative content. Today's two signals show what comes next. Beijing is moving to consolidate that patchwork into one law, and at the same time its creative industries are running generative AI at a scale no other market has reached. The rule book and the content machine are being built at once.

## China's State Council Names a Comprehensive AI Law in Its 2026 Legislative Work Plan

On May 11, 2026, the State Council General Office issued its 2026 Annual Legislative Work Plan, which calls on the government to improve AI governance and accelerate comprehensive legislation for the sound development of AI. As the South China Morning Post reported, this was the first time Chinese authorities used such detailed language to describe planned AI legislation. The 2025 plan said only that authorities would promote legislative work for AI development. The new plan commits to refining legislation across six named domains, data, computing power, algorithms, property rights, cybersecurity, and supply chains, and the National People's Congress has now listed AI legislation as a review item for the third consecutive year.

China can move this from plan to statute quickly because it has years of enforcement experience to draw on. The Cyberspace Administration and the Ministry of Industry and Information Technology have already run sector-specific regimes, so a comprehensive law folds tested rules into one enforceable framework that every ministry, province, and platform follows. National targets of 70 percent AI penetration in key sectors by 2027 give regulators a reason to want a single instrument.

A unified Chinese statute would join the European Union AI Act as the second major comprehensive regime, leaving the United States as the largest economy still governing AI through scattered federal and state measures. Multinationals in China would face one compliance baseline instead of a dozen overlapping rules, and Beijing gains a template it can export through standards bodies and Belt and Road digital agreements. Monday morning, if you sell AI into China, map your compliance against the six named domains and find the gaps before draft text appears.

## China's Short Drama Studios Release 470 Fully AI-Generated Episodes a Day

An average of 470 fully AI-generated short dramas were released every day in January 2026, according to research firm DataEye. The platform FlexTV has halted all traditionally shot productions and moved entirely to AI. Kunlun Tech, parent of the apps DramaWave and FreeReels, now lists more than 1,000 AI titles, and rival StoReels aims to produce 100 AI dramas a month. A North American production that once cost roughly 200,000 US dollars now costs 80 to 90 percent less, and the production cycle has fallen from three or four months to under one.

China can scale this because the short drama industry already reached 6.9 billion US dollars in 2024, passing the country's annual box office for the first time, and platforms select content by performance data rather than creative instinct. Generative AI fits that pipeline exactly, and domestic video models from ByteDance and Kuaishou give Chinese studios a low-cost stack foreign rivals lack.

The global microdrama market reached 11 billion US dollars in 2025, and Omdia projects 14 billion by the end of 2026, with the United States supplying about half of overseas revenue. Chinese studios of roughly 10 people now produce English-language series for US audiences, making short drama China's first mass cultural export built on generative video. Monday morning, if you run a media business, commission one test AI series and benchmark its cost per minute against your live-action output.
