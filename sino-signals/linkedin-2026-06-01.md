What does it look like when one buyer orders the largest fleet of robots in history?

On April 24, State Grid Corporation of China issued its 2026 Embodied Intelligence Development Plan, committing about 6.8 billion yuan (about 1 billion US dollars) to buy roughly 8,500 robots this year. The breakdown is concrete: 5,000 quadruped inspection robot dogs for substations and transmission lines, 500 humanoid live-line work robots for ultra-high-voltage projects, and 3,000 dual-arm inspection units. Suppliers include Unitree, UBTech, Zhipu, Deep Robotics, and Fourier. The remaining 1 billion yuan goes to R&D and talent.

Why it matters in China. State Grid runs an annual intelligence budget near 80 billion yuan, so this order is under 9 percent of one year's spend and easy to repeat. Procurement runs in three phases, a first-quarter pilot, a third-quarter scale-up, and a fourth-quarter top-up. Vendors must meet State Grid's own equipment specification and adapt to its Guangming power model, which locks the supplier field into a single state-buyer standard at national scale.

Global impact. One buyer ordering 8,500 units in a year sets unit-cost and reliability benchmarks no Western utility purchase comes close to, and pulls Unitree, UBTech, and Fourier up a volume curve that lowers their export prices. State Grid projects 500,000 to 800,000 yuan in annual labor savings per unit, a 2 to 3 year payback, five times faster inspection, and an 80 percent cut in safety incidents.

Monday morning. If you run critical infrastructure, price a pilot against your current manual inspection cost and safety record. If you build industrial robots, expect Chinese vendors to arrive with utility-proven references and volume pricing inside a year.
