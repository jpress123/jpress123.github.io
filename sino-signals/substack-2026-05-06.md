## iQiyi Launches Nadou Pro Long-Form AIGC Platform With 117-Actor Digital-Twin Library

On April 20, 2026, at the iQiyi World Conference in Shanghai, iQiyi senior VP Liu Wenfeng announced that 117 partner actors, including Chen Zheyuan, Cheng Lei, Zeng Shunxi, Jiang Long, and Ma Su, had agreed to join the Nadou Pro (纳逗Pro) AI artist library. Their licensed multimodal data builds dedicated digital twins for AIGC long-form productions on a platform that integrates nearly 70 intelligent agents across script evaluation, storyboarding, production design, editing, and distribution. Founder and CEO Gong Yu told the conference that real-shot live-action filmmaking may eventually be classified as intangible cultural heritage. Within 48 hours, actors Zhang Ruoyun, Wang Churan, Li Yitong, and Yu Hewei denied authorising any AI use of their likenesses, prompting iQiyi to clarify that joining the library represents only willingness to negotiate, with each project requiring separate authorisation.

Why China can scale this. iQiyi reaches roughly 130 million daily active users and runs the country's largest premium AIGC long-form pipeline. Tencent Video chairman Sun Zhonghuai forecast that one fully AI-produced premium long-form drama will debut in Q3 2026, anchored by digital-twin libraries of this kind, with another third of long-form film and animation deeply involving AI inside two years.

Global implications. Nadou Pro arrives ahead of any comparable SAG-AFTRA-aligned framework in the United States or Europe. The 48-hour talent backlash signals where consent and revenue frictions will surface globally as Disney, Netflix, and Amazon Prime evaluate AI talent infrastructure. Western studios sourcing Chinese co-productions need to confirm whether on-screen talent has been ingested into a platform digital-twin library and at what consent grade.

## Hangzhou Intermediate People's Court Rules AI Replacement Alone Cannot Justify Worker Dismissal

The Hangzhou Intermediate People's Court has upheld on appeal a lower-court ruling that a Hangzhou tech firm illegally dismissed AI quality-assurance supervisor Zhou (300,000 yuan annual salary) after reassigning him to a lower role with a 40 percent pay cut once an LLM had taken over his function. The intermediate court ruled that AI integration is a strategic choice, not the objective major change required under Article 40 of the Labour Contract Law to terminate without consent, and the alternative role offered failed the reasonable-reassignment standard because of the pay cut. The bench directed employers to prioritise retraining and reasonable redeployment with compensation ahead of punitive downgrades. Caixin Global reported the appellate verdict on April 30, 2026, with Bloomberg, NPR, Fortune, and South China Morning Post following inside three days.

Why China can scale this. The ruling sets binding precedent for Zhejiang province (66 million population, the country's densest AI-startup base outside Beijing) and persuasive authority for parallel intermediate courts in Beijing, Shanghai, and Shenzhen. The AI Plus action plan target of 70 percent AI penetration in key sectors by 2027 puts millions of QA, customer-service, paralegal, and basic-coding roles inside the same legal frame as Zhou's.

Global implications. China is the first major jurisdiction to set a high-court precedent that AI cost savings alone cannot ground a layoff, ahead of comparable test cases in the EU, UK, and US. Multinationals operating in China face a mandatory retraining and reassignment-with-compensation pathway before any AI-driven workforce reduction. AI capex business cases that booked headcount savings as a primary return now depend on redeployment economics ahead of simple headcount removal.

## What ties them together

Both rulings draw the same line from opposite sides. iQiyi's digital-twin library says the human contribution becomes a licensed input the platform recombines at scale. The Hangzhou court says the human contribution cannot be unilaterally severed once that recombination is in production. Together they outline the working contract any China-operating creative studio or enterprise will sign with AI for the next five years, and they preview the contests every Western jurisdiction will run after them.
