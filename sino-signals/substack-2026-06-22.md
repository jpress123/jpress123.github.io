Sino-Signals | Monday, June 22, 2026 — China Writes the First Rulebook for Autonomous Agents

China sets the early reference point on agent accountability while its biotechs turn foundation models into a global drug-discovery business.

Two signals today share one theme: AI is moving from assistant to actor. Beijing is writing the governance layer for agents that decide and execute on their own, and Chinese drug developers are folding large models into the core of how new medicines get designed. Both reset the bar for Western counterparts.

## China Issues First National Guidelines Governing Autonomous AI Agents

On May 8, 2026, the Cyberspace Administration of China, the National Development and Reform Commission, and the Ministry of Industry and Information Technology jointly issued implementation guidelines for the standardized application and innovative development of AI agents, the first national policy written specifically for agentic systems. The guidelines define an agent as a system capable of autonomous perception, memory, decision-making, interaction, and execution, and they require that users keep the final right to be informed and the right to halt the agent. They set four areas of action covering technical foundations and standards, safety and security, 19 priority application scenarios across research, industry, consumption, and public services, and an innovation ecosystem. Sensitive sectors face filing, testing, and product recall measures.

Why China can scale this: the AI plus campaign drives agents into government services, manufacturing, and consumer apps simultaneously, and one central rulebook aligns ministries, platforms, and local governments on the same targets. Naming 19 scenarios tells state buyers and vendors where to deploy first, compressing the path from pilot to national rollout.

Global implications: this is the first national framework built specifically for agents, the technology Western firms are shipping without settled rules on authority and liability. By fixing the user's stop control and a decision-authority boundary, China sets an early reference point for accountability when an autonomous agent acts. Regulators elsewhere now have a template to study or answer.

Monday morning action: define decision-authority limits and a human stop control before scaling any agent, and have legal map liability for autonomous actions now.

## Harbour BioMed Launches Global AI Drug Company With BioMap

On June 21, 2026, Harbour BioMed and BioMap announced they will jointly create MegaStream TechBio, a new AI drug research company built for the global market. The venture combines BioMap's xTrimo, a full-modality life-science foundation model with 268 billion parameters, with Harbour BioMed's large library of fully human antibody data. Harbour founder Wang Jingsong said AI is shifting from an assistant to a core engine in drug development, joining project design and research decisions. The deal lands as ByteDance spun off its AI-pharma unit for independent funding in June and as Roche, Eli Lilly, and AstraZeneca expand their own AI drug platforms.

Why China can scale this: the country hosts a deep bench of antibody developers and foundation-model labs, so a platform-to-platform tie-up can pool proprietary data, compute, and pipelines that single project deals cannot. Domestic AI drug firms including Insilico Medicine, XtalPi, and Jacobio are already entering capital markets, giving the sector funding and a path to validate molecules in clinic.

Global implications: Chinese biotechs already file the majority of global AI drug discovery patents, and a model-scale joint venture aimed at the world market raises the competitive bar for Western pharma. If MegaStream turns its foundation model into clinic-ready candidates, it pressures incumbents on both speed and cost, and out-licensing could reset deal economics for the global pipeline.

Monday morning action: test whether your AI strategy is a bolt-on tool or a core platform, and track Chinese AI drug platforms as licensing partners and competitors, not distant experiments.
