What does AI regulation look like when it arrives as an operating manual instead of a debate?

China's National Financial Regulatory Administration just answered. On June 18 it issued the Guiding Opinions on Safe AI Development and Application in Banking and Insurance, document Jin Fa 2026 No. 8. The framework sets 32 requirements across seven areas: governance architecture, model development, data governance, compute buildout, risk management, capability building, and supervision. Institutions are accountable under a whoever-uses-it-is-responsible principle, and high-risk AI applications must carry human oversight and intervention mechanisms at key stages.

Why this matters in China: the rules bind every bank and insurer in the world's largest banking system at once, turning the entire sector into a single testbed for classification-based AI risk management. Large institutions are directed to export compute services to smaller ones and co-build shared infrastructure, so compliance doubles as capability building. The document implements the State Council's AI Plus action opinion, which puts central-government weight behind enforcement.

For Western leaders, this is one of the first comprehensive AI rulebooks from a major national financial regulator, arriving while US and EU supervisors still work from general-purpose AI law and scattered guidance. Global banks and insurers operating in China must now build lifecycle AI governance to NFRA specifications, and regulators elsewhere gain a concrete template pairing risk tiers with mandatory human-override points and vendor audits.

Monday morning action: chief risk officers should map the 32 requirements against their own AI inventories, since risk tiering, human-intervention points, and supply-chain audits tend to migrate across jurisdictions. Compliance teams with China operations should begin gap assessments now. The document is already in force.
