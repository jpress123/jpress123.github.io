What changes when the US government, not a Chinese lab, sets the number for how far behind China is on frontier AI?

On May 1, 2026, NIST's Center for AI Standards and Innovation (CAISI) published the first independent benchmark-by-benchmark evaluation of DeepSeek V4 Pro, the 1.6 trillion parameter open-weight model DeepSeek released on April 24. Using Item Response Theory across 16 benchmarks and 35 models, CAISI scored V4 Pro at an 800 Elo, lagging GPT-5.5 at 1260, with the US frontier leading by about 8 months.

The headline finding is the lag. The harder finding is the cost line.

V4 Pro is the most capable PRC model CAISI has evaluated, scoring 96 percent on PUMaC 2024, 97 percent on OTIS-AIME-2025, and 90 percent on GPQA-Diamond. At 1.74 US dollars per million input tokens, it beats GPT-5.4 mini on cost in 5 of 7 CAISI benchmarks, ranging from 53 percent cheaper to 41 percent more expensive. V4 Pro drops more than 30 points on long agentic trajectories, where US frontier models hold up.

For Western leaders, this report is now the reference document. European, Southeast Asian and Gulf procurement teams will cite NIST numbers when comparing OpenAI, Anthropic and DeepSeek in tender processes. Frontier-vendor price-to-capability slides built on internal data lose against an official US government table.

Monday morning, three moves. If you set AI vendor strategy, rebuild your price-to-capability tradeoff slide with CAISI's IRT Elo and cost ratios before your next budget cycle. If you sell US frontier models, prepare a CAISI-grounded counter on long-agentic-task performance, where the 30-point V4 Pro drop is your strongest defence. If you run government or regulated procurement, draft your position on whether NIST's IRT scoring becomes your purchase-decision benchmark.
