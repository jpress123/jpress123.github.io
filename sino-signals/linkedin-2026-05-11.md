Is your enterprise agent ready for a Chinese filing audit by year-end?

On May 8, the Cyberspace Administration of China, the National Development and Reform Commission, and the Ministry of Industry and Information Technology jointly issued the Implementation Opinions on the Standardized Application and Innovative Development of Intelligent Agents. It is the first national framework anywhere that binds AI agents to a specific governance regime, and it names 19 typical application scenarios spanning scientific research, industrial development, consumer markets, public well-being, and social governance.

Why China can scale this fast. The Opinions slot into a regulatory stack already in motion. The amended Cybersecurity Law took effect January 1. The Anthropomorphic Interactive AI Services Measures take effect July 15. There are already 748 generative AI services filed at end of 2025. The new agent framework adds three risk layers: endogenous model safety, supply-chain integrity for external components, and application-derived risks such as automated attack, privacy intrusion, and disinformation. Sensitive sectors like smart manufacturing, energy, transportation, financial services, healthcare, judicial services, and public safety face filing, testing, and product recall under joint oversight by cyberspace regulators and sector authorities. Low-risk consumer and office scenarios stay on platform self-regulation.

Global impact. China is now the reference architecture for agent regulation. The EU AI Act agent provisions, UK consultations, and Singapore drafts will all be benchmarked against the operational specificity of the 19 scenarios and the tiered filing model. Multinational AI vendors will need agent-level documentation that specifies decision authority, human override paths, traceability logs, and sector filings before Chinese enterprise procurement opens summer pilots.

Monday morning. If you advise a regulated-industry general counsel, draft an internal policy on agent decision boundaries, user override rights, and audit logs that meets the Implementation Opinions test. Walk your board through how the 19 scenarios compare with the agent guidance you face in the EU and US. Treat the document as your global agent-deployment risk taxonomy.
