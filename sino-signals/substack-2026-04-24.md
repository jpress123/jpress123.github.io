Tsinghua and iFlytek Launch Liangzhi Kaiwu Venture, Capture 10,064 Atom Array and Break Caltech Record

On April 22, Tsinghua University's Institute for Advanced Study and iFlytek unveiled Liangzhi Kaiwu (量智开物) at the 2026 Intelligent Quantum Summit in Beijing. Tsinghua professor Zhai Hui, a co-founder of the venture, disclosed that his atomic quantum computing team had captured 10,064 neutral atoms in a single experiment using fully indigenous hardware. That breaks the previous public record of 6,100 atoms set by Caltech. Liangzhi Kaiwu also released two companion algorithms, Zhui Feng (追风) for rapid rearrangement of large atom arrays and Bian Que (扁鹊), an AI-driven quantum error correction decoder developed jointly with iFlytek's research institute that resolves qubit loss at the algorithm layer rather than through added hardware.

Why China can scale this quickly. iFlytek chairman Liu Qingfeng tied the venture directly to compute and energy pressure, citing daily token calls across Chinese services hitting 140 trillion in March 2026, roughly 1,000 times the early 2024 level. Liangzhi Kaiwu pairs iFlytek's large-model engineering with Tsinghua's atom trapping hardware and is chartered to build agentic quantum computers that use LLMs for autonomous tuning. State media promotion and iFlytek's enterprise distribution give the platform immediate channels.

Global implications. Crossing 10,000 neutral-atom qubits on domestic parts compresses the timeline for fault-tolerant quantum chemistry, materials science, and optimization. Pharma and chemicals firms banking on Western vendors such as QuEra or Atom Computing hitting 10k qubits in 2027 to 2028 should expect narrower lead time. Export controls on quantum hardware also lose leverage when lasers, control systems, and decoders are all built in Beijing.

Monday morning action. Commission a review of your quantum roadmap against the Liangzhi Kaiwu stack and the Zhai Hui group's published work. Identify which drug discovery, materials, or optimization use cases could be benchmarked on a 10,000 atom system.

China's MIIT Pegs National Compute at 1,882 Exaflops, Roughly 6,000 Times What Top500 Tracks

The Ministry of Industry and Information Technology is now reporting China's aggregate national compute capacity at 1,882 exaflops, a figure roughly 6,000 times the combined rating of Chinese systems on the Top500 supercomputer list. The headline blends high-performance supercomputers, provincial computing hubs, cloud data centers, and domestic AI accelerators from Huawei, Cambricon, and Biren into a single pooled number that Western observers cannot readily decompose. Analysts caution that theoretical peak and operating capacity are mixed in the figure.

Why China can scale this. China counts eight state-designated computing hubs, state-backed clouds, and chip fleets that never appear in MLPerf or Top500. Consumer services like ByteDance's Doubao and Tencent's WeChat already route inference through this pool, which is why daily token calls crossed 140 trillion in March. A broader compute base translates into lower inference unit economics and a faster runway for consumer agent deployment.

Global implications. Export controls have been modeled against a compute gap that appears much smaller than public benchmarks suggest. Policy debate in Washington and Brussels is likely to move from chip counts toward energy supply, interconnect bandwidth, and software stack maturity. Consumer AI vendors serving global markets will face Chinese competitors operating on a structurally lower inference cost curve.

Monday morning action. Retire any strategy slide that uses Top500 or hyperscaler capex as a proxy for Chinese AI capacity. Commission a refreshed view that incorporates MIIT data and provincial hub disclosures, then stress-test your pricing and latency assumptions.
