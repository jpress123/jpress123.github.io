What happens to the EV charging debate when a battery maker just builds a national swap grid instead?

On July 1, CATL announced its Choco-SEB battery swap network passed 2,000 operational stations across China, up from 700 in late 2025. As of June 30 the network spans 31 provinces and 180 cities, with over 80 percent coverage in small and medium cities and more than 200 new stations opening every month. CATL targets 3,000 stations in over 190 cities by year end, and has opened its first two stations in Hong Kong.

Why this matters in China: CATL supplies close to half of the country's EV batteries, so it owns the cell chemistry, the swap hardware, and the standards at once. New stations ship with Shenxing supercharging piles, so each site both swaps packs and fast charges. Drivers can earn about 40 yuan a day by returning batteries during peak power pricing, which quietly turns parked packs into distributed grid storage.

Global impact for Western leaders: battery swap converts an EV from a product into an energy service. Unbundling the pack cuts the sticker price and removes charging waits, the exact model most Western automakers walked away from a decade ago. A 2,000-station base with standardized packs pressures global rivals to license CATL's system or watch swap-ready EVs undercut them on upfront cost.

Monday morning: model how a swap network with grid-storage revenue changes total cost of ownership against your own charging bets, then decide whether to license, partner, or compete. Fleet operators running Chinese commercial EVs should price swap contracts now, since downtime savings compound fastest in trucking and taxi cycles.
