What happens to your cloud bill when a competitor starts buying intelligence at factory prices?

At the Alibaba Cloud Summit in Hangzhou on May 20, Alibaba declared itself China's "AI factory." Cloud chief Liu Weiguang framed AI as a new kind of manufacturing that earns revenue through training and inference factories. The company shipped three products as one stack: Qwen3.7-Max, the Zhenwu M890 accelerator from its T-Head chip unit, and the Panjiu AL128 server that links 128 M890 chips into a single rack. In an internal test, Qwen3.7-Max ran 35 hours without human input on an M890 it had never seen in training, made over 1,000 tool calls, and wrote a computing kernel that beat the chip maker's own version tenfold.

Why this matters in China: Alibaba says it is the only company in the country running all five layers of the AI stack, from silicon to agentic applications. Owning the chips, the cloud, and the model lets it tune unit economics across the whole chain and sell compute the way a factory sells output. Tens of thousands of enterprise customers and a state push for homegrown silicon give the integrated stack a captive market while Nvidia access stays constrained.

Global impact: a vertically integrated Chinese stack undercuts the assumption that frontier AI requires US chips and US clouds. The AI-as-manufacturing framing turns compute into an industrial commodity priced for volume, pressuring the margins of Western hyperscalers and chip vendors.

Monday morning: benchmark Alibaba Cloud's training and inference pricing against AWS, Azure, and Google before you renew any multi-year compute contract. War-game what your cost structure looks like if a rival runs on a Chinese full-stack at factory prices, and stress-test any supply plan that depends on a single chip vendor.
