## China's State Council 2026 Legislative Work Plan Confirms Acceleration of a Comprehensive AI Law Covering Data, Compute, Algorithms, Property Rights, Cybersecurity, and Supply Chains

China's State Council issued its 2026 legislative work plan in the second week of May 2026 with a first-of-its-kind commitment to accelerate comprehensive legislation for the sound development of AI. The text names six pillars, data, computing power, algorithms, property rights, cybersecurity, and supply chain security, plus a separate clause covering the regulation of major application scenarios. The National People's Congress simultaneously listed AI legislation as a review item for the third consecutive year. Last year's plan used a single line about promoting AI legislative work, this year's text is the most detailed pronouncement Chinese authorities have ever issued on a planned national AI statute.

China can move from sector-specific rules to one umbrella law because the existing stack already does the work. More than ten regulations, the generative AI measures, deep synthesis rules, recommendation-algorithm rules, the May 8 implementation opinions on intelligent agents, and the July 15 anthropomorphic-interaction measures, give the State Council and NPC a tested working library to consolidate. Hangzhou, Beijing, and Shenzhen have already passed local AI regulations that function as legislative laboratories.

China would become the second major jurisdiction after the EU with a comprehensive horizontal AI law, while the United States continues with sector-specific executive actions and no federal statute. Multinationals already compliant with the EU AI Act will face a second comprehensive regime with overlapping but non-identical risk classifications. The named focus on AI property rights and supply chain security signals the law will reach training-data ownership, model-weight provenance, and chip-to-model documentation, areas where US and EU law remain unsettled.

## DeepSeek Opens First Outside Funding Round at 45 to 50 Billion USD Valuation Led by China Integrated Circuit Industry Investment Fund With Tencent and Alibaba in Talks to Participate

DeepSeek opened its first outside funding round in early May 2026, with the targeted valuation climbing from about 20 billion US dollars to 45 billion US dollars in a few weeks, according to Financial Times and Bloomberg reporting carried by TechCrunch on May 6. The round is led by the China Integrated Circuit Industry Investment Fund, the state vehicle known domestically as the Big Fund, with Tencent and Alibaba in talks to participate. Founder Liang Wenfeng, who controls close to 90 percent of the company, opened the round to fund employee equity after rival labs began poaching DeepSeek researchers, and follow-on reporting puts the target size at up to 50 billion yuan, roughly 7.35 billion US dollars.

DeepSeek can scale because its V4 Pro model already runs natively on Huawei Ascend 950PR silicon, removing the export-controlled Nvidia exposure that constrains every other frontier lab. The Big Fund leading the round binds DeepSeek into China's industrial planning architecture for compute self-sufficiency, while Tencent and Alibaba bring more than a billion monthly active users across WeChat, Mobile QQ, Amap, Taobao, and DingTalk for distribution. The open-weights release pattern means each new V model lands on tens of thousands of Chinese university clusters, hospitals, and startups within hours of release.

A state-led round at 45 to 50 billion US dollars values DeepSeek roughly on par with Anthropic's mid-2025 round and confirms that the comparison set for frontier AI is now Sino-American rather than American-with-Chinese-followers. Western pharma, finance, and government buyers that ran V4 Pro pilots will face renewed export-control scrutiny once a Chinese state investment fund is on the cap table. Open-weights labs in Europe and the Middle East gain a credible non-Western anchor to benchmark against.

## The pattern

Both signals capture the same consolidation move at different layers. The State Council pulls a decade of sector-specific AI rules into one statute. The Big Fund pulls a private champion into the official compute-and-capital plan. Each move locks a horizontal frame around what had been a set of vertical pieces. For Western leaders, the working assumption that Chinese AI is a fast-moving but fragmented opportunity is harder to defend by the end of this week than it was at the start.
